Stock-Flow Dynamics of
College-Educated Labor
in Connecticut

Does Connecticut export its college graduates to Boston and New York — and do local degree-holders raise wages for everyone else?

68% CT grads stay at 1 year
80% MA grads stay at 1 year
85% NY grads stay at 1 year
$32 wage gain per % degree share
RQ 1 Is Connecticut a net exporter of college-educated labor relative to Massachusetts and New York? (Bound et al., 2004)
RQ 2 Does local college-degree concentration improve wages for non-degree workers in CT? (Winters, 2013)

02. data sources

Three federal datasets, linked

All data pulled via public APIs — Census, LEHD, and BLS — covering 2022 cross-sections and PSEO cohorts from 2001–2019.

PSEO

Post-Secondary Employment Outcomes

Links IPEDS graduation records to Unemployment Insurance earnings. Tracks where graduates work at 1, 5, and 10 years post-graduation. R2024Q4 release, cohorts 2001–2019.

⚠ Undercounts federal employees and graduates who move to non-participating states. Cell suppression for small cohorts.

ACS

American Community Survey

2022 one-year estimates for state-level degree attainment (S1501) and labor force participation (S2301). Five-year estimates for county-level analysis to avoid suppression.

⚠ One-year ACS suppresses cells coded as −888,888,888 — affects 25–29 age group and several PUMA observations. Cross-sectional only.

QCEW

Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages

BLS county-level annual averages for all 8 CT counties. Average weekly wages at aggregate level code 70 (all ownerships). Used for county-level spillover analysis.

⚠ Captures economy-wide wages, not specifically non-degree workers. Suppresses cells that would identify individual employers.

03. methods

How we answered the questions

All processing in Python (pandas). Three sequentially numbered notebooks: 01_data_pull → 02_clean → 03_analysis.

01

Graduate retention rate (RQ 1)

From the PSEO flows file, compute r_t = grads_emp_instate_t / grads_emp_t for t ∈ {1, 5, 10} years. Compare CT, MA, and NY at each time horizon. A lower retention rate means more graduates are leaving the state for work.

02

County-level OLS (RQ 2)

Inner join QCEW county wages with ACS five-year degree-share estimates on county name (exact match, all 8 CT counties). Estimate wage_c = α + β · DegreeShare_c + ε. All results are correlational — no causal claim.

03

Causal identification note

Causal identification of the spillover effect would require an instrument for degree concentration or a quasi-experimental design with exogenous variation in local degree share. No such strategy is available here — findings are descriptive.

04. results

What the data shows

Five figures, two research questions answered.

Figure 1  ·  ACS 2022

Degree attainment by age group — CT, MA, NY

CT and MA track closely across age groups. NY lags in younger cohorts, likely reflecting NYC's large immigrant population. The 45–54 peak is consistent across all three states from the 1980s–90s higher-ed expansion. Note the 25–29 group is suppressed in all three states.

Source: ACS 2022 one-year estimates, Subject Table S1501.

Figure 2  ·  PSEO 2024

Graduate production over time

NY dwarfs CT and MA in raw graduate production. CT grew from ~15k to ~25k per cohort 2001–2016.

Figure 3  ·  PSEO 2024

LFP by education level

LFP rises monotonically with education in all three states. CT outperforms NY for workers without a high school diploma (36.5% vs. 27.3%).

Key finding — RQ 1: The figure below shows CT retains only 68.3% of bachelor's graduates at one year — compared to 79.6% for MA and 85.2% for NY. The gap widens over time, suggesting graduates take CT jobs first, then migrate to Boston and NYC as their careers develop.

Figure 4  ·  PSEO flows file 2024  ·  Central finding

Graduate retention rates at 1, 5, and 10 years post-graduation

CT retains 68% at 1 year vs. 80% (MA) and 85% (NY). Gap widens to 60% vs. 70% vs. 75% at 10 years. Source: PSEO flows file, R2024Q4.

Key finding — RQ 2: A 1 pp increase in county degree share is associated with $32 higher average weekly wages (β = 32.27, R² = 0.63, p = 0.018). correlational — Fairfield County's proximity to NYC is a likely confounder. Effect disappears in manufacturing-only wages (R² = 0.16, p = 0.33), suggesting service-sector spillovers drive the pattern.

Fairfield (51.2% degree share, $2,015/wk) vs. Windham (24.3%, $1,027/wk). Relationship is correlational — no causal claim. Source: ACS 5-year 2022; QCEW 2022 annual.

05. takeaway

What this means for Connecticut

Two findings, clearly scoped — one strong, one suggestive.

RQ 1 — Graduate retention

Connecticut retains substantially fewer of its bachelor's graduates than MA or NY at every time horizon, and the gap grows over a decade. This updates Bound et al. (2004) using direct PSEO flow data unavailable to prior work. CT faces a structural challenge competing with Boston and NYC labor markets for its own graduates.

RQ 2 — Spillover wages

County-level evidence is consistent with Winters (2013): higher degree concentration correlates with higher wages. But with only 8 observations and Fairfield County as a NYC-proximity outlier, the causal mechanism is unidentified. The effect is absent in manufacturing wages, pointing to service-sector channels.

Limitations

PSEO undercounts federal-employment graduates. ACS is cross-sectional. County OLS has n = 8 with no controls. PUMA-level LFP analysis showed no statistically significant relationship (p = 0.23), suggesting spillovers operate at finer or different geographic scales than PUMAs capture.

Future work

Individual-level ACS microdata with metro-area fixed effects would better isolate the spillover channel. Extending the retention analysis to specific industries (aerospace, finance) would reveal which sectors CT loses graduates to — and whether targeted policy could change that.
